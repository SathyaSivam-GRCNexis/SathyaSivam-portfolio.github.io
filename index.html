GRC Mindset

How I Think

The principles that drive every risk assessment, control recommendation, and audit I conduct.

01

Risk Before Compliance

I never start with a framework checklist. I start with the question: "What can go wrong, and what's the business impact?" Controls exist to reduce risk — if you don't understand the risk, the control is just paperwork.

02

Control Prioritization

Not all controls carry equal weight. I prioritize based on risk severity, likelihood, and business context. A critical access control gap in a SaaS CRM is a higher priority than a documentation gap in an internal wiki.

03

Business Over Bureaucracy

Compliance should enable the business, not block it. I design controls that integrate into existing workflows — not processes that exist only to satisfy an auditor for two weeks a year.

04

Evidence-Driven, Not Opinion-Driven

Every finding I raise is backed by evidence. Every recommendation is traceable to a specific risk. Audits are objective exercises — not opportunities to second-guess teams without data.

01

SaaS CRM Risk Assessment Using NIST RMF

NIST RMF Risk Assessment SaaS CRM

Context

A multi-tenant SaaS CRM platform handling customer PII, financial records, and communication logs across 15+ geographic regions needed a structured risk assessment to support its expanding compliance obligations.

Problem

No formal risk assessment methodology existed. Risk decisions were reactive — driven by audit findings rather than proactive identification. No risk register, no risk owners, and no consistent scoring.

Approach

Applied the NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF): categorized system assets by data sensitivity, identified threats using STRIDE modeling, assessed likelihood and impact using a 5x5 matrix, and mapped risks to existing controls.

Key Risks Identified

  • Unauthorized access to tenant data via privilege escalation
  • Data residency violations due to uncontrolled cross-region replication
  • Insufficient logging for privileged user actions
  • Third-party integrations without security review

Controls Implemented

  • Role-based access control (RBAC) with quarterly access reviews
  • Data residency tagging and region-locked storage policies
  • Comprehensive audit logging with 90-day retention
  • Vendor risk assessment process for all third-party integrations

Outcome

Established a risk register with 47 identified risks, assigned risk owners across 6 departments, and reduced high-severity unmitigated risks by 68% within two audit cycles. Framework now used as the standard for all product risk assessments.

02

ISO 27001 Gap Assessment for SaaS Platform

ISO 27001 Gap Analysis Remediation

Context

A SaaS platform preparing for ISO 27001:2022 certification needed a comprehensive gap assessment against Annex A controls to identify what was missing before the Stage 1 audit.

Problem

The organization had informal security practices but no documented ISMS. Policies existed in scattered documents, access controls were inconsistent, and incident response had no formal process. Teams assumed compliance was "good enough."

Approach

Conducted a clause-by-clause gap assessment against ISO 27001:2022 (clauses 4-10) and all 93 Annex A controls. Interviewed process owners, reviewed documentation, tested control effectiveness, and scored each control as Conforming, Partial, or Non-Conforming.

Key Gaps Found

  • No Statement of Applicability (SoA) or risk treatment plan
  • Access management lacked periodic review processes
  • Incident management had no classification or escalation criteria
  • Business continuity testing never conducted

Remediation Delivered

  • Developed SoA mapping 93 controls to business justification
  • Implemented quarterly access review process with evidence collection
  • Created incident classification matrix with SLA-based escalation
  • Designed and executed BCP tabletop exercise with documented results

Outcome

Identified 31 non-conformities and 18 partial conformities. All critical gaps remediated within 90 days. Organization achieved ISO 27001:2022 certification on first attempt with zero major non-conformities during the Stage 2 audit.

03

Automated Control Mapping Across Frameworks

Control Mapping Multi-Framework Automation

Context

Managing compliance across ISO 27001, SOC 2, GDPR, HIPAA, SEBI, and OWASP simultaneously — with each framework tracked independently — created massive duplication of effort and inconsistent control evidence.

Problem

Teams were implementing the same control differently for each framework. A single access control requirement appeared as 6 separate tasks. Evidence was collected multiple times. Audit prep took weeks because nothing was cross-referenced.

Approach

Built a unified control mapping system within the CRM GRC Console. Normalized 149 controls across 8 frameworks into common control objectives. Each control links to its framework-specific clause, risk, evidence, and DRI (Directly Responsible Individual).

Challenges Addressed

  • Semantic differences in control language across frameworks
  • Overlapping requirements with different evidence expectations
  • No single source of truth for control status
  • Manual spreadsheet tracking causing version conflicts

Solution Design

  • Common Control Library with framework-specific mappings
  • Single evidence artifact serving multiple framework requirements
  • AI-powered control matching suggesting cross-framework links
  • Real-time compliance dashboard showing coverage per framework

Outcome

Reduced audit preparation time by approximately 60%. Eliminated duplicate evidence collection. Achieved single-pane visibility across all 8 frameworks. Control mapping now maintained by 1 analyst instead of 4, with higher accuracy and consistency.

1. Purpose

This policy establishes requirements for controlling logical and physical access to information assets, ensuring that access is granted based on business need and the principle of least privilege.

2. Scope

Applies to all employees, contractors, and third parties who access the organization's information systems, networks, and data — including SaaS applications, cloud infrastructure, and on-premises systems.

Insights & Thought Leadership

What I Write About

Risk-focused thinking on compliance, security, and GRC — for practitioners who want clarity, not checklists.

01

Top 5 Security Risks in SaaS CRM Systems

SaaS CRM platforms are high-value targets — they store customer PII, financial data, communication logs, and often serve as the single source of truth for sales and support. Yet most organizations treat CRM security as an afterthought.

The five risks I see most frequently:

  • Over-privileged access: Sales reps with admin-level permissions they never needed. No periodic access reviews. RBAC exists on paper but not in practice.
  • Uncontrolled third-party integrations: Marketing tools, analytics platforms, and chat plugins connected to the CRM without security review or data flow mapping.
  • Data residency blind spots: Multi-tenant architectures replicating data across regions without controls, creating GDPR and SEBI exposure.
  • Weak audit logging: CRM platforms logging user actions but not privileged operations — making incident investigation impossible.
  • No data classification: All customer data treated equally, whether it's a company name or a financial record, leading to insufficient protection where it matters most.

The fix isn't more tools — it's risk assessment. Map the data, identify what can go wrong, and implement controls proportional to the impact.

02

Common ISO 27001 Implementation Mistakes

After supporting multiple ISO 27001 implementations and conducting dozens of internal audits, the same mistakes appear repeatedly. They're not technical failures — they're strategic ones.

  • Starting with controls instead of risk: Teams jump to implementing Annex A controls without conducting a proper risk assessment. The SoA becomes a checklist rather than a risk-driven document.
  • Treating the ISMS as a project, not a system: Implementation is done as a one-time effort. After certification, the ISMS stagnates. No continual improvement. No regular management reviews.
  • Documentation theater: Policies written to satisfy auditors, not to guide employees. If the people doing the work haven't read the policy, it's not a control — it's a document.
  • Ignoring Clause 4 (Context): Organizations skip stakeholder analysis and scope definition, then struggle when auditors ask why certain assets or locations are excluded.
  • Confusing compliance with security: Passing an audit doesn't mean you're secure. ISO 27001 is a framework for managing risk — not a guarantee that risk has been eliminated.

The organizations that get it right treat ISO 27001 as a business tool for managing information risk — not a badge to display on their website.

03

Why Control Mapping Fails in Organizations

Control mapping should save time. In practice, it often creates more confusion. Here's why most control mapping efforts fail — and what actually works.

  • Mapping at the wrong level: Teams map framework clauses 1:1 without understanding that ISO 27001 A.5.15 (Access Control) and SOC 2 CC6.1 (Logical Access) don't have identical scope — they overlap, but they're not the same.
  • No common control language: Without a normalized control library, teams end up with spreadsheets where the same control is described differently for each framework, making maintenance impossible.
  • Ignoring evidence differences: Two frameworks may accept the same control, but require different evidence. Mapping the control without mapping the evidence creates audit surprises.
  • Static spreadsheets: Control maps built in Excel and shared via email. No version control. No ownership. No update triggers when frameworks change.
  • No risk linkage: Controls mapped to frameworks but not to the risks they mitigate. Without risk context, there's no way to prioritize which gaps matter most.

Effective control mapping requires three things: a common control taxonomy, framework-specific evidence requirements, and a live system (not a spreadsheet) that tracks status in real time.
