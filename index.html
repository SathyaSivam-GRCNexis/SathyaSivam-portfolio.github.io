GRC Mindset

How I Think

The principles that guide how I approach every security review, risk assessment, and audit.

01

Risk Before Compliance

I never start with a checklist. I start with the question: "What can go wrong, and how would it affect the business?" When I assessed risks for a SaaS platform across 5 categories (Engineering, Operational, Vendor, Legal, Business), every security decision was driven by a scored risk — not by which rule came next in a standards document.

02

Severity-Based Prioritization

Not all gaps are equally urgent. When I found 6 types of compliance gaps across 12 frameworks, I classified each by severity — Critical, High, Medium, Low. A missing security safeguard protecting customer data is Critical. A missing documentation link is Low. Resources go where the risk is highest.

03

Governance by Design

Security should be built into how teams work — not added after the fact. When I designed security checkpoints for a SaaS platform's development process, I placed compliance gates at every phase with 5 trigger types. This means security is checked during development, not discovered as a problem after release.

04

Evidence-Driven, Not Opinion-Driven

Every finding I raise is backed by proof. When tracking compliance across 434 controls, I score evidence on 4 dimensions — completeness, freshness, format, and coverage — with quality grades from A to F. If the evidence quality is poor, the control gets flagged regardless of what the responsible person claims. Audits should be objective, not based on assumptions.

05

Translate Risk Into Business Language

A risk list that leadership can't understand is a risk list that gets ignored. When presenting to stakeholders, I translate technical security risks into business terms — potential revenue impact, regulatory fines, and customer trust. The risk heat map I designed isn't just for analysts — it gives executives a quick view of where the organization stands, what's being addressed, and what needs urgent attention.

01

Enterprise Risk Assessment for a Multi-Framework SaaS Platform

Risk Assessment 5x5 Matrix SaaS

Context

A cloud software platform (SaaS) certified across 12 compliance frameworks (SOC 2, ISO 27001, GDPR, HIPAA, and 8 others) needed a structured way to identify and track security risks. The platform handles sensitive customer data — making risk identification and scoring critical for passing audits.

Problem

No formal list of risks existed. Risk decisions were reactive — the team only dealt with risks when auditors pointed them out, not before. There was no consistent way to score how serious a risk was, no one was formally responsible for each risk, and there was no way to track whether actions taken were actually reducing the danger over time.

Approach

Designed a structured risk assessment covering 5 risk categories: Engineering, Operational, Vendor, Legal & Regulatory, and Business. Each risk is scored on a 5x5 matrix — measuring how likely it is to happen (1-5) multiplied by how much damage it could cause (1-5) — giving a score from 1 to 25. Risks are auto-classified into 5 severity levels (Critical, High, Medium, Low, Negligible) and assigned to a named owner with a plan and target date to address them.

Safeguards Put in Place

  • Role-based access control (who can see and do what) with 4 levels and segregation of duties (no one person can both submit and approve)
  • Per-risk tracking with an assigned owner, due dates, and residual risk calculation (the remaining risk after safeguards are applied)
  • Weekly automated reminders for any risk not reviewed in 90+ days — preventing risks from going stale
  • A visual 5x5 heat map showing all risks by severity — for quick executive and auditor review

Outcome

Established a structured risk register with 18 identified risks across all 5 categories. Each risk has a named owner, documented action plan, and residual risk tracking. Automated weekly reviews ensure no risk goes stale. The visual heat map gives auditors and leadership clear visibility into the platform's risk posture — moving risk management from reactive audit findings to proactive, scored, and tracked governance.

02

ISO 27001 & SOC 2 Gap Assessment for SaaS Platform

ISO 27001 SOC 2 Gap Analysis

Context

A SaaS platform managing 434 security controls across 12 compliance frameworks needed a way to find compliance weaknesses before external auditors did. Controls (specific security rules or safeguards) were tracked across ISO 27001, SOC 2, GDPR, HIPAA, and 8 other standards — but there was no systematic way to detect gaps, rank their severity, or track fixes.

Problem

Security practices existed informally, but gaps were discovered inconsistently. Some controls were marked "implemented" without supporting evidence. Others had expired evidence or no one assigned to maintain them. These problems only surfaced during audits — when it was too late to fix them efficiently.

Approach

Designed a gap assessment methodology that detects 6 distinct gap types: Missing Implementation (control not in place), Missing Evidence (no proof it works), Partial Coverage (only partly done), Expired Evidence (proof is outdated), No Owner (nobody responsible), and No Documentation (no written procedure). Each gap is classified by severity (Critical, High, Medium, Low) and assigned to someone with a target fix date.

Key Gaps Found

  • 165 controls across frameworks were "Not Implemented" — the most common gap type requiring prioritized action
  • 163 controls were "Partially Implemented" — needing additional evidence or process completion
  • Controls with no assigned owner — creating accountability gaps that auditors would flag
  • Evidence linked to controls but past its expiry date — creating a false sense of readiness

Remediation Delivered

  • Structured fix workflow: Open → In Progress → Remediated → Closed, with deadline tracking at each stage
  • Severity-based prioritization ensuring Critical and High gaps are addressed first
  • Automated notifications to the assigned person and their manager when gap status changes
  • Comparative assessments over time — ISO 27001 (42 gaps found) and SOC 2 (17 gaps found) run as initial baselines to measure improvement

Outcome

Established a repeatable gap assessment process across all 12 frameworks. Of 434 controls, 99 are fully implemented, 163 are partially implemented, and 165 are identified for remediation — each with assigned owners, severity classification, and tracked deadlines. Gap assessments run per-framework with comparative tracking to measure improvement over time. Auditors now receive a clear gap status report instead of discovering issues during audit.

03

Cross-Framework Control Mapping for 12 Compliance Standards

Control Mapping Multi-Framework 175 Mappings

Context

A SaaS platform certified across 12 frameworks was managing 434 security controls independently per framework. Each framework had its own tracking, evidence collection, and audit preparation — creating massive duplication of effort. Think of it like maintaining 12 separate to-do lists that overlap significantly, but nobody knows which items are shared.

Problem

A single security requirement (like "control who can access the system") appeared as separate tasks for ISO 27001, SOC 2, GDPR, and HIPAA. Evidence was being collected multiple times for what was essentially the same rule. Audit preparation was slow because nothing was cross-referenced. Teams didn't know which security controls overlapped across frameworks.

Approach

Created 175 cross-framework control mappings across 5 primary frameworks (SOC 2, ISO 27001, GDPR, HIPAA, ISO 27701), classifying each link as one of three types: Equivalent (controls are essentially the same), Partial Overlap (similar but not identical scope), or Related (same topic, different specific requirements). This means one piece of evidence can now satisfy multiple frameworks at once.

Challenges Addressed

  • Controls that look different across frameworks but actually cover the same ground — mapped with the right relationship type
  • Same security concept described differently in different standards — normalized into a common language
  • Different evidence expectations per framework for the same underlying control
  • Static spreadsheet tracking causing version conflicts and outdated information

Solution Design

  • 175 bidirectional control mappings with 3 relationship types (Equivalent, Partial Overlap, Related)
  • Single evidence artifact serving multiple framework requirements — submit once, satisfy many
  • Per-framework compliance scoring so each framework's status is tracked independently while sharing common controls
  • Framework-pair matrix showing where frameworks overlap most and least — helping prioritize evidence collection

Outcome

Established 175 cross-framework mappings across the platform's 12 frameworks, eliminating duplicate evidence collection for overlapping controls. Each of the 434 controls now links to its framework-specific rule, associated risk, evidence, and responsible person. Audit preparation improved because auditors can see cross-framework coverage in a single view. The mapping is maintained as a living system — not a static spreadsheet.

04

Development Lifecycle Security — Compliance by Design for SaaS Releases

SDLC Governance Compliance by Design SaaS Platform

Context

A SaaS platform certified across 12 compliance frameworks ships frequent feature updates. Every release needs to meet security, privacy, and regulatory requirements — but compliance was being treated as something checked after release, not during development. Think of it like only checking if a building meets fire codes after it's already built.

Problem

No structured security review existed within the development process. Compliance checks happened after features shipped — not during development. There was no system to track which security checkpoints each release had passed or failed. Problems were discovered during audits, not during development — when they're much more expensive and disruptive to fix.

Approach

Designed a 7-phase security pipeline for the development lifecycle — with mandatory checkpoints at each stage. Each checkpoint has trigger-based assessments (data, privacy, security, regulatory, AI) that are evaluated per release. Results are tracked as pass/fail with risk ratings and grouped by release version so auditors can see the full picture.

The 7 Security Phases

  • Requirements: Security and privacy needs identified before development begins
  • Design: Security built into the architecture — identifying threats and selecting the right safeguards
  • Development: Secure coding practices verified — proper data handling, encryption, input validation
  • Testing: Security testing confirms access controls, logging, and data handling work correctly
  • Deployment: Formal approval and change management sign-off before going live
  • Maintenance: Ongoing monitoring and security patches after release
  • Decommission: Secure data disposal when features are retired

How It Works in Practice

  • Each platform release is logged with checkpoints across relevant phases
  • Each checkpoint is assessed per release — pass or fail, with risk rating and notes
  • Failed assessments trigger automated daily alerts so nothing gets missed
  • A per-release view groups all assessments so auditors can verify compliance for each version
  • 5 trigger types (data, privacy, security, regulatory, AI) determine which checkpoints apply

Outcome

Established a fully operational security pipeline for the development lifecycle — 7 phases, mandatory checkpoints per phase, and trigger-based assessments per release. Auditors now have a per-release compliance view that satisfies SOC 2, ISO 27001, and ISO 9001 requirements for secure development and change management. Compliance shifted from a post-release problem to a built-in development gate — security by design, not by afterthought.
